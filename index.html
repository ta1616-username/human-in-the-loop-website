Linguistic Constraints & Rewriting

Transforming Generic Politeness into Golden Responses

The Challenge

AI systems often default to generic politeness patterns—hedging, over-qualifications, repetitive reassurance—that, whilst inoffensive, fail to meet users where they actually are. The model responds as though addressing a courtroom rather than a confused person needing help.

The Approach

We designed an annotation framework around linguistic constraints: identifying which politeness markers were serving genuine epistemic humility, and which were just defensive filler. The framework distinguished between:

  • Necessary hedging – acknowledging genuine uncertainty or knowledge boundaries
  • Defensive filler – habitual qualifications that distance the model from its own response
  • Contextual appropriateness – whether the user's emotional or cognitive state calls for directness or careful scaffolding

The Outcome

Fine-tuned models responded with genuine directness when directness served the user, and with patient scaffolding when the user was visibly overwhelmed. Responses felt less like template completions and more like thinking alongside someone. User satisfaction increased not because the model became more deferential, but because it became more honest about its own confidence levels.

Key Learning

Politeness isn't the goal—clarity and authenticity are. A model that says "I don't know" clearly is more useful than one that says "Whilst it is conceivable that one might consider it possible that..."

Multi-Step Reasoning & Truthfulness

Teaching AI to Think Before Answering

The Challenge

Models often generate plausible-sounding responses that skip over genuine reasoning steps. When pressed on how they "arrived" at an answer, they confabulate justifications. The gap between fluency and actual reasoning was significant, and users couldn't tell the difference.

The Approach

We borrowed from teaching methodology: before a model answers, it should "show its working." We annotated examples using metaphor-based reasoning patterns:

  • The scout metaphor – exploring the problem space before committing to a path
  • The map-maker metaphor – marking uncertainty as explicitly as certainty
  • The student metaphor – asking clarifying questions about what the user actually needs

Crucially, models were trained to flag when they didn't have sufficient information—not to hedge, but to explicitly identify the gap.

The Outcome

Fine-tuned models showed visible reasoning chains. When they said "I'm uncertain," they could explain why. Users reported greater confidence in model outputs precisely because the model's doubt was transparent. Fewer users tried to use the system for tasks where the model correctly identified it was unequipped.

Key Learning

Extended reasoning isn't just about better answers—it's about epistemic honesty. A model that can tell you *why* it's uncertain is more trustworthy than one that confidently generates plausible guesses.

Empathy Calibration & Safety

Validating Appropriate Boundaries

The Challenge

Training models to be empathetic is necessary; training them to understand the *boundaries* of that empathy is harder. A model that recognises distress but responds by attempting therapy, or that offers excessive reassurance to someone disclosing trauma, crosses into unsafe territory. Empathy without professional boundaries causes harm.

The Approach

We developed an annotation framework centred on professional boundaries in human-centred fields (teaching, counselling, support work). The framework distinguished between:

  • Recognising vulnerability – detecting when a user is distressed, grieving, or in crisis
  • Appropriate response – validation and signposting, not attempted intervention
  • Knowing when to refer – identifying situations where professional human support is necessary
  • Avoiding over-investment – preventing the model from over-personalising or creating artificial intimacy

Critical insight: recognising someone's pain *doesn't* require the model to take ownership of solving it.

The Outcome

Fine-tuned models responded to distress with genuine acknowledgement and clear signposting to professional resources. Users felt heard without being over-managed. The model's limitations became a feature, not a flaw: it clearly stated when professional support was necessary. Crisis support hotlines were referenced appropriately, not as a catch-all but as a specific resource for specific needs.

Key Learning

Empathy and boundaries aren't in tension—they're interdependent. A model that boundaries makes space for genuine empathy; one without them creates false intimacy and potential harm. Professional capacity doesn't mean coldness; it means clarity about what help a system can and cannot offer.
